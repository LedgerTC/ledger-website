Who Ledger is, and why this page exists

Ledger Trade & Capital is a direct private lender for licensed builders and experienced real estate developers. We finance ground-up construction, DSCR rental, and fix-and-flip loans up to $5M, in 42 states plus Washington, D.C. We are not a broker, not a marketplace, and not a correspondent. Every loan on our book is underwritten, closed, and serviced by our team.

The shape of the business is intentionally narrow. Real builders, real projects, business-purpose only, with a workflow that respects how a construction draw cycle actually moves. 48-hour draws, named underwriter, 21-day close target, first-time builders welcome on the vertical construction product.

This comparison exists because Ledger and Temple View show up in overlapping Google searches, and borrowers ask us the same question every week: which of us is the right fit for their deal? We'd rather answer it in writing.

Temple View Capital, headquartered in Bethesda, Maryland, is a private lender offering ground-up construction, DSCR, rehab (fix-and-flip), and a distinct 2nd Mortgage Rental product. Their headline leverage matches Ledger's on the two most compared programs: 90% LTC on construction and 75% LTV on DSCR purchase. Temple View's DSCR program is also notable for its aggressive 0.75x minimum DSCR on long-term rentals, which is roughly a half-turn looser than what most lenders publish.

Where the two lenders part ways is on the construction borrower profile and on state footprint. Temple View's ground-up program is explicitly for Experienced Investors, with LLC/LP/Corp borrower-entity structures only. Their marketing claims “Nationwide Lending” with an asterisk, but the site publicly discloses licenses in only six states: California, Arizona, Minnesota, Utah, Oregon, and Nevada. Outside of those states, coverage is on a by-exception basis, which makes pre-application comparison difficult.

Where each lender fits better

We're not going to tell you Ledger is better on every axis. Here's the honest read, based on what each of us publishes.

Where Ledger fits better

  • First-time builders are eligible. Temple View's ground-up program is explicitly restricted to Experienced Investors. Ledger runs a dedicated first-time builder program with underwriting and pricing calibrated for operators on their first vertical project.
  • Published nationwide footprint. Ledger lists 42 states plus DC by name. Temple View markets “Nationwide Lending” but publicly discloses licenses in only six states (CA, AZ, MN, UT, OR, NV), with everything else “by exception.” If your deal is outside Temple View's disclosed six, you can't know before you apply whether they'll lend there.
  • Higher DSCR loan ceiling. Up to $5M on Ledger DSCR vs. Temple View's $2M cap. Relevant on higher-value single-asset rentals.
  • Higher F&F loan ceiling. $5M vs. $4M. Modest on small deals, decisive at the top of the range.
  • Individual borrowers considered on construction. Temple View's ground-up product requires LLC/LP/Corp structures. Ledger will consider individual borrowers on the construction and rehab programs.
  • Published starting rate. 8.875% on construction and F&F. Temple View doesn't publish a rate floor on any of the three comparable products.
  • No prepay on construction or F&F, posted. Ledger publishes no prepay penalty. Temple View publishes that a prepay penalty applies to DSCR (amount not stated) and doesn't specify on the other products.

Where Temple View fits better

  • Aggressive DSCR floor. 0.75x minimum DSCR on long-term rentals is roughly a half-turn looser than the 1.0x floor most lenders publish. If your target asset pencils below break-even on rent (a narrow but real edge case), Temple View is built for it.
  • Longer posted construction term. 12–24 months vs. Ledger's 12–18. For complex builds with longer timelines, that extra window matters.
  • Longer posted rehab term. Up to 24 months on Rehab Loans vs. Ledger's 15–18 month F&F term.
  • Lower DSCR minimum loan size. $55K floor on DSCR vs. Ledger's larger minimum. Makes small single-asset rentals financeable where they'd be too small for us.
  • Distinct 2nd Mortgage Rental product. Temple View has a posted second-position product for borrowers who want to keep their existing first mortgage and add a second-position rental loan. Ledger doesn't offer second-position lending.
  • Lower F&F minimum. $75K floor vs. Ledger's $125K. Relevant on smaller urban or rural rehab deals.
  • Investor-focused product design. The borrower-entity-only structure and experience gate on construction are a deliberate box. Active investors running entity-structured deals may appreciate a lender built for that profile.
